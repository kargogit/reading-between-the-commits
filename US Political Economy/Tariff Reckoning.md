# The Great Tariff Reversal: Inside the $166 Billion Refund Unleashing a New Economic and Legal Reality

**Executive Summary**

On April 20, 2026, the United States government activated a massive, unprecedented bureaucratic engine: the launch of a federal portal to return $166 billion in unlawfully collected tariffs to over 330,000 businesses. This rollout, overseen by U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), follows a landmark February Supreme Court ruling that struck down the foundational legal authority President Donald Trump used to launch his sweeping 2025 tariff agenda. 

While the refund portal represents a watershed moment in administrative law and trade policy, its macroeconomic impact is deeply fractured. Corporations are eagerly lining up for multi-billion-dollar payouts, but consumers—who ultimately absorbed the inflationary shock of the tariffs through higher prices—should not expect a check in the mail. Meanwhile, the White House has already pivoted to alternative legal frameworks to keep its protectionist policies alive, ensuring that the battle over trade, executive power, and consumer costs is far from over.

This report talks about the legal, administrative, economic, and consumer realities of the post-IEEPA tariff landscape, drawing on current data, court filings, and expert analysis.

---

## I. The Legal Catalyst: *Learning Resources v. Trump*

The current refund mechanism was triggered by the Supreme Court’s February 20, 2026, 6-3 decision in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*. The Court ruled that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)—a 1977 statute granting the president power to regulate commerce during national emergencies—did not authorize President Trump to impose sweeping "reciprocal" and fentanyl-related tariffs in 2025.

**The Fractured Majority:** While six justices agreed IEEPA was insufficient, the legal reasoning split the Court:
*   **The Major Questions Doctrine:** Chief Justice John Roberts, joined by Justices Neil Gorsuch and Amy Coney Barrett, relied on the "major questions doctrine," arguing that such a "big-time policy call" requiring vast economic authority requires clear congressional authorization. (Gorsuch and Barrett separately debated whether the doctrine stems from constitutional separation of powers or is simply a tool for statutory text interpretation).
*   **Straight Statutory Construction:** The Court’s three Democratic appointees (Justices Sonia Sotomayor, Elena Kagan, and Ketanji Brown Jackson) concurred in the judgment but rejected the major questions framework, arguing the case could be resolved through standard statutory interpretation.
*   **The Dissent:** Justice Brett Kavanaugh, joined by Justices Clarence Thomas and Samuel Alito, argued IEEPA did authorize the tariffs and warned that the majority’s ruling would create a "mess" regarding the refunding of collected duties. Kavanaugh also posited a "foreign affairs exception" to the major questions doctrine, which Roberts sharply rejected, noting that the Constitution grants Congress, not the President, the power to impose peacetime tariffs.

**Unresolved Legal Questions:** The ruling left the door open for future litigation. The Court did not decide whether IEEPA allows the president to "deal with" unusual threats via non-tariff regulations, nor did it rule on whether the president's national emergency declarations under IEEPA are reviewable by courts.

## II. The Refund Apparatus: The CAPE Portal

Following the Supreme Court’s ruling, the legal battle shifted to the U.S. Court of International Trade (CIT). Judge Richard Eaton ordered the government to refund all IEEPA duties with interest, brushing aside CBP's arguments that reviewing tens of millions of records would be administratively impossible. "We live in the age of computers," Eaton noted.

On April 20, CBP launched the Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries (CAPE) tool within the Automated Commercial Environment (ACE) portal at 8:00 AM ET.

**How the System Works:**
*   **Eligibility:** Only importers of record or their authorized customs brokers who directly paid the IEEPA duties can apply. Consumers are not eligible to submit claims.
*   **Requirements:** Importers must have an established ACE Portal account, provide bank account details for direct deposit, and submit "CAPE Declarations" listing the specific goods and entry documents. Accuracy is paramount; legal experts warn that a single misidentified entry can cause an entire claim to be rejected.
*   **Phased Rollout:** The government is processing refunds in phases. Phase One is restricted to "unliquidated entries" (where tariffs were estimated but not finalized) and entries within 80 days of final accounting. Older, more complex entries will be processed later.
*   **Timeline:** CBP estimates that approved claims will be paid out in 60 to 90 days. However, 10 of 25 CFOs surveyed by CNBC expect the process to take a year or longer.

**The Scale:** According to CBP executive Brandon Lord, as of March 4, over 330,000 importers made more than 53 million entries, paying roughly $166 billion in IEEPA tariffs. By April 14, 56,497 importers had registered for electronic payments, making them eligible for an initial tranche of $127 billion (including interest, which is accruing at an estimated $650 million per month).

## III. The Consumer Paradox: Who Actually Gets the Money?

While the Supreme Court and CIT ordered the return of $166 billion, the economic reality of who ultimately benefits is stark. Tariffs are legally paid by importers, but the costs are largely passed down to consumers through higher prices. The refund system, however, reimburses only the importers.

**Corporate Retention:** A quarterly CNBC CFO Council survey reveals that none of the 25 major corporate CFOs surveyed intend to directly share tariff refunds with customers. Of the 12 CFOs planning to apply for refunds, six explicitly stated they would not pass on any portion, while seven were unsure. 

Economists and analysts overwhelmingly agree with this trajectory:
*   **Mark Zandi, Moody's:** Viewed the refunds as "compensation" for the hefty toll on U.S. businesses, predicting companies will "hold on to those [refunds]."
*   **Paul Donovan, UBS:** Noted it "seems unlikely anyone will rush to lower prices."
*   **Goldman Sachs:** Analysts project consumer prices will not decline, noting tariffs are still expected to raise costs by another 0.1% in 2026 after adding 0.7% to inflation over the previous ten months. Prices historically rise quickly in response to tariffs but fall slowly when tariffs are cut.
*   **Alex Durante, Tax Foundation:** Does not expect an "immediate urge to pass [refunds] back to consumers," as firms anticipate new tariffs under alternative legal authorities.

**The Exceptions:** A handful of companies have publicly committed to passing savings downstream. Costco CEO Ron Vachris pledged to turn refunds into "lower prices and better values." Delivery giants FedEx and UPS—which collected tariffs directly from consumers at the point of delivery—have also committed to refunding customers once they receive CBP payouts.

**Consumer Legal Recourse:** Class-action lawsuits are currently winding through the legal system against major retailers and brands (including Essilor Luxottica) to force them to reimburse shoppers. On the legislative front, bills like the *Tariff Refunds for Working Families Act* and the *Trump Tariff Rebate Act* have been introduced, but analysts give them a remote chance of passing, as Republicans would have to admit a policy mistake, and Democrats view the tariff inflation as a potent midterm election issue.

## IV. The Economic Fallout and Macroeconomic Data

According to the Yale Budget Lab, the 2025 tariffs raised an estimated $214.7 billion in inflation-adjusted customs revenue above the 2022–2024 average. With $165 billion subject to refunds, the net revenue gain from the tariff experiment drops to just $49.7 billion.

**Price Pass-Through:** The Yale Budget Lab finds that tariffs have unequivocally driven up consumer prices. Personal Consumption Expenditure (PCE) core goods prices rose 2.3% during 2025 (through January), compared to a -0.1% change the prior year. The implied "spot" passthrough of tariffs to imported consumer goods prices ranges from 46% to 86% for core goods, and 51% to 115% for durables, depending on the counterfactual methodology used. This aligns with the theory that importers initially absorb costs through compressed margins, but eventually pass them forward as margin compression becomes unsustainable.

**Trade Dynamics and the Dollar:** The U.S. experienced a massive import surge in early 2025 as businesses stockpiled ahead of the tariffs, pushing imports 22% above trend by March. By January 2026, imports had fallen 6.3% below trend as firms worked through stockpiles. Furthermore, contrary to traditional economic models that predict a tariff-levying country's currency will appreciate, the U.S. dollar weakened by 7.6% since December 2024, exacerbating the cost of all imports. 

**Labor Market:** While aggregate employment remains stable, tariff-exposed industries show signs of weakness, trending 0.9% below pre-2025 projections, though manufacturing specifically has not seen significant job losses.

## V. The Administration’s Pivot: New Tariffs, New Legal Battles

The Trump administration has not abandoned its tariff agenda. Immediately following the Supreme Court ruling, the President invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, imposing a 10% across-the-board tariff (later raised to 15%), valid for up to 150 days to address balance-of-payments deficits. 

The administration has also initiated investigations under Section 301(b) of the Trade Act against 15 countries and the EU regarding excess manufacturing capacity, and investigations into 60 countries regarding forced labor. If these investigations conclude that unfair practices exist, they could trigger new, legally distinct tariffs.

However, these alternative avenues face immediate headwinds. The CIT has already noted that Section 122 explicitly limits presidential power regarding balance-of-payments deficits, and new lawsuits allege the President has not met Section 122's statutory prerequisites. Furthermore, Chief Justice Roberts explicitly warned in his majority opinion that alternative statutes "contain various combinations of procedural prerequisites... and limits on the duration, amount, and scope of the tariffs they authorize."

President Trump has publicly raged against the Supreme Court decision, baselessly claiming the ruling affirmed his "absolute right to charge tariffs in another form." Legal experts note the Court said no such thing, and a petition for rehearing—due by March 17—faces astronomically low odds of success, as the Court has not granted such a rehearing on an argued case since 1965.

---

## VI. Conclusion

The activation of the CAPE portal marks the beginning of the largest trade-related refund in U.S. history, rectifying what the highest court deemed an unconstitutional overreach of executive power. Yet, the structural realities of the economy dictate that the $166 billion windfall will largely repair corporate balance sheets rather than consumer wallets. 

Simultaneously, the legal and economic architecture of U.S. trade remains in a state of profound flux. The administration's rapid deployment of alternative legal justifications for new tariffs ensures that the trade war is not over; it has merely migrated to a new statutory battlefield. For businesses, the refund process offers vital liquidity, but the looming threat of Section 122 and Section 301 tariffs guarantees that the supply chain disruptions and inflationary pressures that characterized 2025 are far from resolved.
